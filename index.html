About Hui Hui Gwan

A Humble Endeavor to Reconnect Two Ancient Worlds

Hui Hui Gwan (回回館) — literally the "House of the Hui Hui" — takes its name from the historical term used in East Asia to refer to Muslim communities. In Korea, these communities once thrived.

On this platform, we bring together — in a thoughtful and careful manner — writings, photographs, works of art, designs, historical notes, and cultural reflections relating to the shared heritage of Islam and Korea. We are not here to lecture, but to spark curiosity and warm hearts through beauty, storytelling, and cultural connection.

What Does 回回 Mean?

回回 (Hui Hui / 회회) was the historical term used across East Asia to refer to Muslims — derived from the Uyghur word for Muslim communities. In Korean records from the Goryeo Dynasty (918–1392), these people were documented as traders, astronomers, healers, and court officials who helped shape the peninsula's golden era.

A Thousand-Year Journey

Islam in Korea: From the Silk Road to Seoul

Trace the journey of Muslim travelers, merchants, scholars, and families who left their mark on Korean history across a millennium.

845 CE
First Encounters: Arab Merchants on the Korean Coast
Early references in Arabic geographic texts by writers such as Ibn Khurdādhbih mention a land called "al-Sīla" (新羅 — Silla), describing Korean ports as destinations for Arab and Persian maritime traders along the eastern Silk Road.
Maritime Trade
1024 CE
The First Official Muslim Delegation Arrives in Goryeo
During the reign of King Hyeonjong (현종), a large group of Muslim traders — referred to as Daesik (大食, from the Arabic Tajir — merchant) — officially arrive in the Goryeo Kingdom. A second group follows the next year. Many settle permanently.
Goryeo Dynasty
13th Century
The Hui Hui Era: Muslim Officials in the Goryeo Court
Under Mongol-Yuan influence, Central Asian Muslims (known as Hui Hui / 回回) arrive in Korea as administrators, scholars, and envoys. Among them: Jang Sun-ryong (장순룡), a Uyghur Muslim who becomes a high-ranking court official, marries a Korean woman, and founds the Deoksu Jang clan (덕수 장씨) — a Korean family that persists to this day.
Royal Court
1274–1356
Ye-gung (禮宮): The First Mosques on Korean Soil
Muslim communities in the capital Gaeseong (개성) build Ye-gung (예궁 — ceremonial halls, effectively mosques) for communal prayer. They maintain their faith while integrating into Korean society, contributing knowledge of Islamic astronomy, medicine, and calendar systems.
Cultural Heritage
14th Century
쌍화점 (Ssanghwajeom): Muslims in Goryeo Pop Culture
The famous Goryeo-era song Ssanghwajeom (쌍화점 — "The Dumpling Shop") portrays Muslim merchants running shops in the capital: "When visiting Ssanghwajeom to buy Ssanghwa, a Hoehoe (Muslim) grabbed my wrist." The song reflects how deeply Muslim traders were woven into everyday Goryeo life.
Literature & Arts
1392–1427
Joseon Transition: The Quiet Disappearance
The Joseon Dynasty ushers in strict Neo-Confucian rule. In 1427, King Sejong (세종대왕) orders Muslims to abandon their traditional headgear, close their Ye-gung, and conform to Confucian norms. The visible practice of Islam in Korea gradually fades — but the bloodlines, surnames, and cultural imprints remain.
Joseon Dynasty
1976
Seoul Central Mosque: Islam Returns to Korea
After centuries of absence, the Seoul Central Mosque opens in Itaewon (이태원) — Korea's first purpose-built mosque. Today, Korea is home to an estimated 200,000 Muslims, including Korean converts and immigrant communities, continuing a connection that began over a millennium ago.
Modern Era
